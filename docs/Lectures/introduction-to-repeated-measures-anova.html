Definition and Purpose of Repeated Measures ANOVA

Repeated Measures ANOVA is a statistical test used to examine the changes in a continuous dependent variable across multiple measurements over time or under different conditions. It is an extension of One-Way ANOVA but accounts for the correlation between repeated measures on the same subjects.

Key Differences Between Repeated Measures ANOVA and One-Way ANOVA

  • One-Way ANOVA: Evaluates the effect of a single independent variable on a continuous outcome.
  • Repeated Measures ANOVA: Evaluates the effect of a single or multiple within-subject factors over time, accounting for the correlation between repeated measurements.

Understanding the Structure of a Repeated Measures Design

In a Repeated Measures design, each participant is measured multiple times under different conditions or over time. The design includes: - Within-Subjects Factors: Variables measured repeatedly on the same subjects. - Levels: The different conditions or time points for each within-subjects factor.

Example Structure

  • Within-Subjects Factor: Time
  • Levels: Baseline, Week 1, Week 2, Week 3

Sphericity Assumption

Sphericity refers to the assumption that the variances of the differences between all combinations of related groups are equal. If violated, it can lead to biased F-statistics and increased Type I errors.

Greenhouse-Geisser Correction

The Greenhouse-Geisser correction adjusts the degrees of freedom to correct for violations of sphericity, providing a more conservative estimate.

Calculation of SS (Sum of Squares) and MS (Mean Squares)

Sum of Squares

  • Total Sum of Squares (SST): \[ SST = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{t} (Y_{ij} - \bar{Y})^2 \] where ( Y_{ij} ) is each individual observation, and ( {Y} ) is the overall mean.

  • Sum of Squares Within (SSW): \[ SSW = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{t} (Y_{ij} - \bar{Y}_{i\cdot})^2 \] where ( {Y}_{i} ) is the mean of all measurements for subject ( i ).

  • Sum of Squares Between (SSB): \[ SSB = \sum_{j=1}^{t} n_j (\bar{Y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{Y})^2 \] where ( {Y}_{j} ) is the mean for level ( j ), and ( n_j ) is the number of observations at level ( j ).

Mean Squares

  • Mean Square Within (MSW): \[ MSW = \frac{SSW}{(n - 1)(t - 1)} \]

  • Mean Square Between (MSB): \[ MSB = \frac{SSB}{t - 1} \]

Hypothesis Testing for Main Effects and Interaction

Hypotheses

  • Main Effect of Within-Subjects Factor:
    • Null Hypothesis (( H_0 )): No difference in the means across levels.
    • Alternative Hypothesis (( H_1 )): At least one level has a different mean.
  • Interaction Effect:
    • Null Hypothesis (( H_0 )): No interaction between within-subjects factors.
    • Alternative Hypothesis (( H_1 )): An interaction exists between factors.

F-Ratio Calculations

  • Main Effect of Within-Subjects Factor: \[ F = \frac{MSB}{MSW} \]

Interpretation

  • Compare the F-ratio to the critical value from the F-distribution.
  • If the calculated F-ratio is greater than the critical value, reject the null hypothesis.

Effect Sizes and Significance

Eta Squared (( ^2 ))

Represents the proportion of the total variance explained by the within-subjects factor.

  • Within-Subjects Factor: \[ \eta^2 = \frac{SSB}{SST} \]

Bonferroni Correction

A post-hoc correction used to reduce Type I errors when multiple comparisons are made. When conducting multiple tests, the risk of a false positive (Type I error) increases significantly. For a detailed explanation of why this happens, see the section on The Problem of Multiple Comparisons in the ANOVA lecture.

Formula

\[ \alpha_{\text{corrected}} = \frac{\alpha}{m} \] where ( m ) is the number of comparisons.

Checking Assumptions of Repeated Measures ANOVA

Sphericity

  • Use Mauchly’s test to check the assumption of sphericity.

Normality

  • Use Q-Q plots to check the normality of residuals.

Homogeneity of Variances

  • Ensure variances are approximately equal across conditions.

Examples of Repeated Measures ANOVA in Nursing Research

Example 1: Evaluating Blood Pressure Changes Over Time

  • Research Question: How does blood pressure change over a 3-week intervention period?
  • Within-Subjects Factor: Time
  • Levels: Baseline, Week 1, Week 3
  • Response Variable: Blood Pressure

download example data (CSV)

# Example in R
# Simulated data
set.seed(123)
data <- data.frame(
  Subject = rep(1:20, each = 3),
  Time = factor(rep(c("Baseline", "Week1", "Week3"), times = 20)),
  BP = rnorm(60, mean = rep(c(120, 115, 110), each = 20), sd = 10)
)

# Repeated Measures ANOVA
install.packages("ez")
library(ez)
anova_results <- ezANOVA(
  data = data,
  dv = BP,
  wid = Subject,
  within = Time
)
print(anova_results)
